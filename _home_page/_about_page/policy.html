MFAH Digital Collections Policy


Purpose & Mission

The libraries of the Museum of Fine Arts, Houston are dedicated to providing access to its special collections through imaging and metadata creation. We are committed to supporting the scholarly needs of new audiences as well as our existing communities, including staff, scholars, artists, students, and the public as they pursue unique or distinctive elements of our research collections.

The libraries will prioritize materials that support museum research needs while secondarily supporting the needs of the broader art research community. We will oversee the selection, creation, delivery, and preservation of these digital collections, while welcoming input concerning the selection process to meet patron demands. As appropriate, the libraries will partner with other museum departments and external entities to support these efforts.


‌Scope

Materials considered for digitization are items held in the collections of the Hirsch and Powell Libraries. We will also provide access to born digital materials created by the MFAH and by local entities that are relevant to the museum’s research needs.


‌Audience & Outreach

The digital collections are created to support those conducting research associated with the museum’s collections. By expanding access to materials solely available on-site, the digital collections will reach a much wider audience, especially those unable to visit the libraries in person.

The presence of these digital collections will enhance discovery, access, and use of our rare materials and can be used to promote other library materials and services to the public.

Further, the creation of digital collections is a way to unite disparate collections into a singular virtual location even if physically separated within the institution.


‌Collection Development

The libraries will focus on unique and significant content that supports institutional memory and history, contributes to existing projects and ongoing research, and highlights museum and

library collection strengths. This will also include materials that would benefit from value-added enhancements such as metadata or searchable text.


‌Selection Criteria

Evaluating resources involves many factors. Some considerations include:

  • Significance: Materials that support areas of long-term or emerging interest to the museum.

  • Availability: Materials that are not widely available or accessible but important to research and scholarship.

  • Equity: Materials that amplify traditionally underrepresented voices in the existing collection.

  • Condition & Preservation: Materials with limited access due to their physical condition will be prioritized for digitization as time, funding, and equipment availability allow.

    • Evaluate if digitization would alleviate preservation concerns and increase access to materials that are otherwise restricted for use.

    • Evaluate if digitization would further damage materials and, if so, consider alternative means of access.


‌Copyright Permissions & Responsibility

Most materials considered for digitization will fall into one of the following categories:

  • Works in the public domain.

  • Works under a valid open access or Creative Commons license.

  • Works for which the museum holds the copyright.

  • Works for which we have obtained permission to digitize and make available.

  • Works that we can make available through fair use.

  • Works deemed to be orphan works (i.e., works for which the copyright holder cannot be located).


    • ‌Considerations & Limitations

    The library will not re-capture content that has already been digitized, meets modern specifications and is available publicly for download from a respected repository, unless the nature of the libraries’ material is markedly different from the version available. For example, if available versions are low resolution, black and white scans, or standard editions, where the

    libraries’ items have added value such as extra-illustration, marginalia, or rarer editions, the libraries may consider re-capturing content.

    Respected repositories include, but are not limited to, HathiTrust, the Center for Research Libraries, the Internet Archive, and Google Books.

    Certain collections may be deemed too labor intensive or complex to digitize in full for the following reasons:

    • Timeline and timeframe: the scope and depth of a collection may be unrealistic to complete alongside regular workloads.

    • Technical requirements: available methods and equipment may be insufficient to properly capture materials at the specified quality.

    • Funding: Additional funding for equipment, project assistants, or outsourcing is necessary.
